RONALD

In 2020 I independently ideated, researched, designed and pitched a new product to solve various problems within Oryon Develop, the CPD events branch of Oryon’s umbrella.

Why?

The shift to online courses, delivered via webinar, necessitated by the Covid-19 pandemic was actually a success story for Oryon Develop. Having been seconded for a month to manage these events whilst the majority of Oryon was on furlough, I experienced some issues we were having with the events first-hand, and became determined to fix them and to grow the business even further.

What?

RONALD was conceived as a four-stage product. RONALD 0.1 is the first step towards automating our processes and bringing our brand back into the onboarding process for webinars. RONALD 0.2 is a move to bring the webinars behind our SaaS community password wall, to protect our content, give us more branding control and, most importantly, to ease the UX of joining a webinar. RONALD 0.3 is an MVP test (which is currently running) to show the demand for on-demand courses and content. RONALD 1.0 is the finished, validated product!

Competitor research

Making an in-depth study of the competitive landscape helped me to understand how competitors were offering online courses.

Product research

Comparing the different offerings for webinar features and, importantly, platform integrations helped me to propose staying with our current live webinar platform provider. However, this research led me to recommend moving recorded events to Vimeo, as their integration with our platform would be easier, and their video playback controls were superior.

Roadmapping, user stories and feature prioritising

I wireframed and produced low-fi prototypes for the various iterations of RONALD, using Marvel and Balsamiq. I listed out all the potential features and met with stakeholders and developers to decide on their priority, using the T-shirt sizing method. I created several user stories, dependent on the use case of the product, to elucidate for stakeholders.

I also created a roadmap for the versions, taking the time to walk stakeholders through it and to make sure that it aligned with the overall roadmap and vision for the company.

Sprint planning and Product Ownership

As this was 'my baby', I was the Product Owner for the development. This meant that I was responsible and accountable for the product backlog, delegating the sprint backlog to the developers.

Pivot / re-ordering

As so often happens with agile product development, the project evolved, and will certainly keep evolving!

Having assessed the market and the potential effectiveness of offering on-demand courses, we decided to push RONALD 0.3 forward, creating an MVP by using recordings of previous courses to test demand. This test is currently running on our live site. After a few months, we will be able to show the demand for recorded courses, and from these results can determine the level of recommended investment in the type of course in the future.

General Assembly Product Management Capstone Project

In December 2020 I completed a 6 day Product Management course at General Assembly. A fantastic week of consolidating knowledge and learning new things was geared around producing this capstone project.

Proof of concept

The problem we identified was that loyalty schemes are difficult to manage, leading to a loss of potential reward points when using the scheme is too much effort for the user. To validate this assumption, we started by collating customer feedback with qualitative data. Interviewing 15 people gave us a good grounding on which to develop the product, confirming our assumption.

MVP testing

The next thing we did was to create a Minimum Viable Product. We created a prototype of a package, showing the product, and asked survey respondents whether they would be interested in the product. With 74% of respondents indicating interest, we further validated our assumptions.

Features, user stories and product roadmap

The next day, we focused on planning out the user journey through the app, prioritising features and creating user stories to help us do so. Having agreed on priorities, we created our product roadmap and used Balsamiq to wireframe the app. Having completed user testing on our coursemates and some independent parties, we made some adjustments.

Product pitch

The next thing to do was work on our presentation to key stakeholders. This involved measuring and estimating the market size and share, using the TAM, SAM and SOM principle. We definied our USP against the competitive landscape, and walked stakeholders through our wireframed product.

Laying out our measurews for success, for which we used the pirate metrics of Acquisition, Activation, Revenue, Retention and Referral, we defined our target and left stakeholders in no doubt as to the potential of the product.
